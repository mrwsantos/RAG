# Episode 4: False attribution and Carl Sim


Carl Sim and False Attribution.

For nearly a decade, a friend of mine received royalties from a song
that was printed in a songbook used by a school.

Every year, the book was reprinted

and he got a check in the mail

He didn't think too much of it until

on a whim, he asked to see a copy

to find out what other songs were included in the collection

The book arrived and he scanned the index

turned to the page for his song

only to find that the song wasn't his

It had the same title as a piece he'd written

and his name was there

but the melody, lyrics, all of that was someone else's work

Needless to say, when he let the school know

that was the last year he received royalties for that work

I think, in this situation, it was a genuine case of mistaken identity

confusion over two songs with the same title

But false attribution

in other words, when one person puts another person's name to a work

when they weren't involved

can be harmful reputationally and financially

for the falsely attributed creator

The law around copyright isn't just about copying.

It also sets out some moral rights you have in your work, including

your right to be correctly attributed

and your right not to be falsely attributed when you didn't make a work.

These are sort of two halves of the same bigger thing,

but for the story I'm about to tell, we'll need to look at false
attribution first.

Section 102 of the Copyright Act sets out that attribution means an
express or implied statement

as to the identity of the author of the work or the director of the
film.

A person has the right not to have a literary, dramatic, musical, or
artistic work falsely attributed to him or her as the author

and not to have a film falsely attributed to him or her as director.

All of which brings me to the story of Carl Sim.

Carl, with a K, Sim, was New Zealand's most well-known art forger,

mostly because he's the only person to have been convicted of art
forgery in this country.

Born in Mangaueka in 1923, Carl was quite a character.

His artistic abilities came to light while he was studying at Palmerston
North Technical College.

Looking at works of masters in any creative discipline

and considering how they were made can be a useful part of the learning
process,

and at the technical college, Sim was encouraged to copy the work of
masters like Charles F. Goldie

to help develop his own talents and skill set.

But something happened which ignited a trajectory for Sim.

Some of his copies were inadvertently auctioned off as originals,

and he saw a golden opportunity.

He began a decades-long career in forgery.

Then, when he was in his 40s and running a wine and antique shop in
Foxton,

he sold a forged C.F. Goldie from the shop premises

and he realized that the antique shop lent his forgeries a sense of
authenticity.

From this point on, he was prolific.

He copied works by at least 50 different artists,

including lots of prominent local artists,

Colin McCann, Rita Angus, Francis Hodgkins, and of course, Charles F.
Goldie.

How do we know?

His sister told journalist Chris Hyde that she had found a handwritten
list

tucked inside a dictionary of artist's signatures.

And when she asked Sim about it, he said,

those are just the ones I remember.

In 1985, everything came to a head.

The number of artworks Sim was selling from his shop made the police
suspicious.

And, adding to their suspicions,

spelling mistakes were found in signatures on several of the paintings.

Sim was caught. The short story is that he was tried and convicted on 40
counts of forgery.

He was fined $1,000 and sentenced to 200 hours of community service,
which included painting the

Foxton Town Hall and the public toilets. But that's not where Sim's
story ends. He continued

to quietly thumb his nose at the establishment, changing his legal name
to Carl with a C,

Theodore Goldie, so that he could sign his work, C.F. Goldie, and avoid
future lawsuits.

And for a while he seems to have flown under the radar because he didn't
pop up again in the news

until 1999. That year, with the help of an associate, a man named Gary
Skinner,

it seems he managed to get two forgeries of New Zealand artist Charles
Heafy's work

listed through auction house done by Sloan. Before the works were
listed,

Sim had sent photocopies of them to the curator of pictorial collections
at the Hocken Library

at the University of Otago. The curator had written back giving their
opinion that one of

the works likely depicted Charles Hefe's wife-to-be. Armed with this,
Gary Skinner got in touch with

the auction house and claimed to have inherited the paintings from his
father. The curator's letter

was used as verification to support the work's authenticity. And they
might have got away with it,

But it seems that Sim was unable to resist talking about the forgeries.

He apparently gave a speech to the Avondale Rotary Club,

boasting about his efforts, and word got around.

The works were pulled from the auction,

though neither Sim nor Skinner were charged.

Both denied knowledge of the particulars of the affair.

The stories around Sim are colourful,

full of twists and turns, thick with thieves, art historians, galleries,

and those unwittingly along for the ride.

Verifying all the details would be a full-time job,

and I suspect there's more than one tall tale.

In 2003, Sim wrote a book about his life, titled As Good As Goldie.

It was co-authored by writer Tim Wilson and published by Hora Moore
Beckett, now Hachette.

It's out of print now, but over on Wilson's bio on the Academy of New
Zealand Literature website,

It's described as a runaway bestseller because every word was true, even
the lies.

When Sim died in 2013, the Herald interviewed his sister.

She said,

He didn't do it for money or anything.

He did it to beat the establishment, because arty people can get a bit
snooty.

But beating them was his main aim.

Here's my view.

I think, for Sim, it was a game.

Maybe it felt exciting or entertaining to dupe people,

especially when they were considered experts.

Whatever the reason, Goldie's work rode on false attribution,

and as a result, his forgeries deflated the value of the work of others.

Whether you see Sim as a lovable rogue or as a deleterious thief,

forgery is not a victimless crime.

Sim was forging and selling art at a time when some of the artists he
copied,

artists like Rita Angus and Colin McCann, were creating work.

It's hard to assess the actual financial impact his actions might have
had

on the value of works by the original artists or the art market.

And clearly, the cost to any institution researching and analysing one
of Sim's works

to determine provenance or authenticity would also be notable.

Remember Section 102 of the Copyright Act?

The law says it's your right not to be identified as a creator of a work
you didn't make.

It's a right that's tied to your creative reputation, and it's also
about authenticity.

It means you can point to the law if you find yourself on the other end
of a similar situation.

As I mentioned before we got into SIM,

the other moral right that fits neatly together with the right against
false attribution is the right of attribution.

Section 94 of the Copyright Act sets this out.

Right to be identified as author or director.

Subject to section 97, the author of a literary, dramatic, musical or
artistic work that is a copyright work has the right to be identified as
the author of the work.

And the director of a film that is a copyright work has the right to be
identified as the director of the work.

The legislation goes on to give a lot of scenarios in which this right
is exercised, mostly to do with publishing, exhibiting, communicating
and showing works.

and that includes on sculptures and buildings.

Basically, and this does seem obvious,

when you make a creative work,

you have the right to be correctly identified as its creator.

And this is something that you have to assert in writing.

In other words, the way you wish to be recognized as your work's creator

should be written down and displayed with your work.

The legislation also sets out that that responsibility is on the
creator.

If you don't alert people in some way,

whether that's through a copyright notice, a plaque on the wall, or some
other identifying mark,

they're not at fault if they neglect to credit you. You can use whatever
name you like. In fact,

the Act says you can specify a pseudonym, or initials, or some other
particular form of

identification. And then it goes on to say, and I'm paraphrasing, that
if you don't specify any of

those, then any reasonable form of identification may be used. So if you
want to go by TJ or

Bugman or some sort of unique symbol, go for it. But make sure you post
this all over your website

and social pages or do something else in writing to let people know
exactly how you want to be

attributed. Friends, we work really hard on our creative endeavors and
it's only fair that we're

able to be identified correctly, something the legislation supports by
setting this out as a

right. Like false attribution, the right of attribution is also about
reputation as an artist

and authenticity. It's about making sure people know that you made your
work. It's about making

it possible for people to find you if they have questions or want to
buy, commission or license

work or engage with you and it in some way. It's about giving credit
where credit is due.
