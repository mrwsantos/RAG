# Where copyright does not apply

**Question:** What is a 'substantial part' and why does it matter for copyright?


**Answer:** Copyright issues do not arise unless there is use of a 'substantial part' of a copyright work. The use of names, titles and headlines is an exception --- copying these individual aspects usually will not infringe copyright in the whole work as it would not be considered a substantial part. Generally not as stand-alone works, as they are considered so commonplace and unoriginal that copyright is simply not ascribed to them. Short stand-alone texts such as slogans or tweets will often not give rise to copyright issues either. For most types of works, copyright lasts until fifty years from the end of the year in which the author dies. For typographical works, copyright lasts for 25 years from the end of the year of publication. Other types of copyright material have slightly different periods of protection. Once copyright expires, the material is sometimes referred to as being in the 'public domain'. Anyone may copy or publish a public domain work without the need to get permission. However, if a public domain work is republished, the publisher acquires a new copyright in the typographical layout of that particular edition. Under the Copyright Act there is no copyright in: Parliamentary Bills, Acts and regulations; bylaws; Parliamentary debates; Select Committee reports; court and tribunal judgements; and reports of Royal commissions, commissions of inquiry, ministerial or statutory inquiries. Yes. The format in which excluded material is published may still be protected by copyright as a typographical arrangement. Copying the published format of such material may in some cases infringe the publisher's copyright, even though the underlying content is not protected.
