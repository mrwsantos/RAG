# Episode 2: The copyright symbol

Nau mai, haere mai. Welcome to Know Your Copyrights, the podcast where
we dive into how creative rights work for creative people in Aotearoa,
New Zealand. Ko Karen Workman, tōku ingoa. Ko Taku Mahi ki Copyright
Licensing New Zealand. Ko Te Kaifakahaere for Kapa, Creative Rights
Educator. Over this series, I'll take you through some real stories of
copyright and creative work to help explain the legal stuff and some
practical considerations too. Today's copyright kōrero covers some
general copyright information, but it's not legal advice. If you've got
a copyright problem, the best thing you can do is to get in touch with a
lawyer for their advice on your specific situation. We've all seen it
somewhere, sometime. Perhaps the little C in a circle showed up after
every song in the jacket of your cassette tapes. Once you'd unfolded all
10 pages of the jacket, just so you could read and meticulously learn
the lyrics for all these new songs. Maybe that's just me. And I've
definitely dated myself now. On sound recordings, you might see the
letter P in a circle, indicating copyright in the sound recording, with
the C used for the other material, like song lyrics and other words,
photos and illustrations. The copyright symbol, or the word copyright,
or the abbreviation COPR, can be found all over the place, on creative
works of every type, books, websites, visual art, software, even some
packaged goods. It's everywhere. But did you know that the symbol
doesn't have to be present for copyright to exist? The Byrne Convention,
the international treaty governing copyright that most countries have
agreed to, does not require the copyright symbol to be present for
copyright to apply to a work. Instead, copyright is automatic. It comes
into existence at the same time as the creative work itself. So why does
the symbol even exist? For centuries, establishing ownership and
authenticity has been important to creators. You might think it's become
more so with the rise of the internet, but there's a long history of
putting a mark on a creative work. Signatures on artwork from the
Renaissance period helped distinguish one artist from another, and seals
on ancient Chinese works were used to signify both the author and all
subsequent owners. Way back in the 1660s, Scotland had the Aberdeen
Almanac, a highly successful book introduced to the public by printer
Edward Rabban. It had eight sections, including a calendar listing fairs
and markets, useful tables with information about post towns, post
masters, stagecoaches and carriers to and from Aberdeen, and multiple
lists of people, for example, members of parliament, establishments,
institutions, and principal inhabitants of local counties and more.
Hugely popular and created with the help of mathematicians from King's
College, The book was the market leader of its day. Of course, you can
guess what happened, as so often does with any other useful popular
thing, copycats. Almanacs printed in Glasgow by Robert Sanders and in
Edinburgh claimed to contain calculations by Aberdeen mathematicians or
to be Aberdeen authorized. In 1674, the printer of the authentic
Aberdeen almanac, John Forbes, who was the successor to the first
printer, Edward Rabanne, stated in the almanac, No almanacs are from
Aberdeen, but where their arms are to be seen, since printers from the
Burras use the Burra Code of Arms as a symbol of copyright and
authenticity. The offending came to a head in 1684. That year Forbes,
fuming, printed a phrase in the almanac, If counterfeit, then hang for
it. Obviously, someone was at the end of their rope, so to speak. At any
rate, Forbes prosecuted the printers of the fake editions successfully
on two counts. First, that he was in use and possession of printing
yearly an almanac as printer of the town and college of Aberdeen. And
second, on the basis that Sanders had attempted to forge the coat of
arms, an infringement which was viewed as particularly brazen and wrong.
So, humans have been marking their ownership and the rights attached to
ownership of creative works forever and a day. The circled C symbol was
introduced in American legislation through the Copyright Act of 1909.
Before that, copyright holders were required to use the full word
copyright, or the abbreviated COPR. Artists did not like this. They
wanted their name on the picture to be the most prominent text without
unnecessary words. So the US government replaced the long inscription
with the symbol. And today that copyright symbol is recognised and used
all over the world. But do you have to use it on your own creative work
for your rights in it to be recognised? Well, as I mentioned earlier,
the copyright symbol doesn't have to be on a work for copyright
protection to apply. In Aotearoa, New Zealand, copyright protection for
a creative work automatically applies so long as your work meets three
criteria. First, it must be new and original. Second, it must be in a
fixed and tangible form. And third, it must have been made by an
identifiable creator. And there's no official place in Aotearoa New
Zealand to register for copyright protection. The law, by default,
protects the work the moment it meets those three criteria. Thanks to
the Bern Convention, automatic copyright protection for any creative
work exists in many other countries too. So, no copyright symbol is
needed, but should you use it? Well, yes. It's best practice to use it
on all your work. And a good, clear format is copyright symbol, followed
by your name and the date. Putting this on your work lets other people
know that the rights in it belong to you. It also means that you're easy
to identify and to find, which is useful, especially if someone else
wants to ask permission to reproduce, adapt, display or distribute your
work, rights which only belong to the copyright owner. The name you use
alongside the symbol doesn't have to be your legal name either. It could
be a pseudonym or a stage name or whatever you choose, and the date
shows the year of creation or first publication. The presence of that
symbol also means that if someone uses your work without your permission
and attempts to defend themselves by saying, I didn't know it was
copyright protected, it's unlikely the courts will give any weight to
that claim. Another best practice tip is to keep a work history. This
helps to show authenticity, process, and when your creation originated.
And it's useful, particularly if you need to enforce your rights or
prove ownership at any time. Those sketches and early drafts of your
work can help support and prove your ownership if there's ever a
question of who copied who, so don't throw them all out. I know creators
who throw sketches away once they've completed the final work. And while
you don't have to keep everything, I'm not encouraging hoarding, filing
a couple of the pivotal ones is a good idea. On the other side of
copyright best practice, what should you do if you want to use a work
made by someone else, but it doesn't have a visible copyright symbol or
other copyright information. Well, hopefully you know from everything
I've just said that the absence of the copyright symbol doesn't mean
it's open and free to use. Although here's a side note, if you're keen
to let other people know your work is free and open, a Creative Commons
license can be a useful way of showing this. Very occasionally, you
might come across a true orphan work, one with no traceable origin or
creator. But often, the lack of copyright information is through
oversight or negligence by the creator or the caretaker, or occasionally
because someone has intentionally deleted the copyright notice. If you
want to use a work that doesn't have a copyright symbol, you need to do
whatever you can to track down the owner so you can get the right
permissions. This might require some sleuthing, most likely through web
searching, phone calls, and sending out a bunch of emails to try and
find the original creator. If you're really unable to find information
about who owns it, and you decide to go ahead and use the work, keep a
record of your efforts, a log of calls, conversations and emails and so
on, so that if you need to, you can show that you diligently tried to
find the owner. Being able to prove your good intentions and efforts to
do the right thing will go a long way in settling an accidental
infringement situation. One way to think of the copyright symbol is to
imagine your created works, your books, your screenplays, your visual
art, etc. as a house that you've built from scratch. You designed it one
room at a time, you sourced the materials, rolled up your sleeves and
got to work. You built the house and you keep adding to it. A bedroom
here, a bathroom there, some landscaping and so on. Now other people
really like the house that you're building but they keep popping in
without an invitation, they take your furniture, they're throwing
parties and making changes like concreting over your vegetable garden.
In this slightly far-fetched scenario, to show everyone that your place
belongs to you, you might put up a sign at the front gate saying private
property. It's not absolutely necessary, the sign doesn't change the
fact that the house belongs to you, but it might be a practical step to
help discourage uninvited visitors. And ultimately, that's how the
copyright symbol functions too. It's a sign that says the rights to use
your property belong to someone. In other words, you. Listen, your work
has value. The choices around where and how your work can be used should
be in your hands. and the copyright symbol is all about reminding people
that those choices belong to you, that it's your right to use your
property the way that you want to.
