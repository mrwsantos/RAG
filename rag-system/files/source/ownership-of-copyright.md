# Ownership of copyright

**Question:** Who owns the copyright in a work?


**Answer:** In most cases, the author of a work is the first owner of its copyright. Copyright arises automatically when an original work is recorded in some form, and no registration or copyright notice is needed. However, authorship and ownership are different things. There are important exceptions to the general rule. Where a work is created in the course of employment, copyright belongs to the employer. For certain types of commissioned works, including photographs, paintings, drawings, diagrams, maps, charts, films, sound recordings, and computer programs, the person who commissioned and paid for the work owns the copyright. Where a work is created by or for the Crown, Crown copyright applies. These default rules can be varied by agreement: an employment contract may state that the employee retains copyright in certain works, and a commissioning agreement may allow the creator to retain copyright. There can be more than one copyright owner, for example where two people collaborate on a work. Copyright, as a property right, can be bought, sold, gifted, or transferred. Moral rights, however, are personal to the creator and cannot be transferred.
