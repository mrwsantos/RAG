# When you need to ask permission

**Question:** When do you generally need to get permission to use third party copyright material?


**Answer:** You generally need permission when you intend to publish your work, include it in an online research repository, or use it for purposes outside your own education or research. Particular care is needed whenever you upload third party copyright material to the internet or communicate it to the public. Yes. Statutory permissions for educational and research purposes (such as examinations and assignments) do not apply if you publish or communicate your work to the public. Depositing a thesis in an institution's online research repository makes it a publication and requires permission for any third party copyright material included. Yes. If you have collaborated with someone else to create your work, you will need to get permission from the other copyright owners before you copy, publish, or do any of the other copyright restricted acts --- unless your use is covered by a statutory exception. No. You need to get permission for any additional or subsequent use. For example, if you have permission to reproduce and incorporate text in a screenplay, you would still need separate permission to perform and record that work. You need to be careful. If you copy from a work that is still under copyright protection in another country and you intend to distribute your work in that country or post it on a publicly accessible website, you will need to get permission from the copyright owner in that country.
