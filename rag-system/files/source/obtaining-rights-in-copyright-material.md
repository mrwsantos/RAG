# Obtaining rights in copyright material

**Question:** How can a publisher or organisation obtain the rights to use copyright material?


**Answer:** There are two main ways to obtain rights in copyright material: assignment and licensing. An assignment is the transfer of copyright ownership. Like other property, copyright can be bought, sold, gifted, or inherited. Any assignment must be in writing and signed by the person assigning copyright. Once assigned, the new owner holds exclusive rights for the full copyright term, even if calculated by reference to the original creator's lifetime. Rights can be assigned partially, limited to specific formats, territories, or purposes. A licence is permission to use copyright material without transferring ownership. Licences can be exclusive (where only the licensee holds those rights, with the same legal standing as the copyright owner) or non-exclusive (where multiple licensees can hold the same rights). In some circumstances a licence can be implied from the situation: issuing a media release, for example, generally implies permission to communicate its contents. Where possible, rights agreements should be in writing. A formal legal agreement is not always necessary but a written record, such as an exchange of emails, helps avoid disputes. Collective management organisations such as CLNZ offer an alternative for publishers and rights users: they allow multiple works to be licensed through a single organisation rather than requiring individual negotiations with each copyright owner.
