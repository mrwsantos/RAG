# Are fan art, fan fiction and cosplay copyright infringement?

Great question – and the answer is...technically, yes. But don’t put
down your pens just yet! When you’re creating work that borrows
obviously and substantially from existing work of other creators without
express permission, it’s pretty likely you’re breaking the law. But
oftentimes, fan work is tolerated, if not encouraged.

Fan works are generally new stories and new artwork set in, or derived
from an existing fictional world. People are expanding a universe whose
elements have been established by a more prominent creator. To tie the
new works to the existing universe usually means using identifiable
elements such as names, places, character descriptions, and other
details. These elements are copyright protected, so any unauthorized
application is by definition, illegal.

However, fan works are tolerated for many reasons, including a lack of
financial competition, the value of world-building and keeping a fan
base engaged, and generosity or goodwill towards fans. It’s all mostly
harmless, and driven by fans who are hungry to consume more material
than the original author/s could probably generate. In the same way,
cosplayers, who build costumes to “play” or pay tribute to universe
characters, are often celebrated by the original creators, who love to
see fans interacting with the shows. Could you get sued by the original
creator, for using their copyrighted material to create stories and art?
Sure, but unless you are selling those works, it’s unlikely the original
creator will enforce their rights, since there’s no financial gain
involved, and there’s a kind of unspoken social contract that no one’s
getting sued for non-commercial fan work.

Where it becomes a problem is when fan works have a direct impact on the
financial potential of the original creator (eg. if the fan artist tries
to sell without permission or license,) or when the new works are using
trademarked elements, (such as logos and exactly replicated images,) or
reflect on the original works in a derogatory way that harms the
reputation of the original creator.

With the rise of the print-on-demand industry, there are more
opportunities to create and monetize, but printing fan work is legally
problematic. Cosplayers can turn to boutique fabric printers to design
something as close to the original character’s design elements as they
can, but sometimes those patterns are registered as trademarks for the
characters. Selling that fabric clearly infringes on the trademark and
copyright of a company that may have intentions of producing and selling
costumes on the mass market, so the print on demand fabric store will
likely receive a cease and desist notice. Companies like RedBubble have
implemented recent agreements with a limited number of brands, and a
process for approving designs before printing, which is a step towards
resolving the complexities of fan art and infringement.

In short – yes, fan work can be considered, and is often clearly,
infringement of copyright. But for some companies, and many original
creators, the payoff for allowing fan work to flourish is a heightened
fan engagement and loyalty, which is worth way more in goodwill than
chasing people off the front lawn.
