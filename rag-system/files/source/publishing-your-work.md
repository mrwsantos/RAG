# Publishing your work

**Question:** What do I need to know about copyright before signing a publishing contract?


**Answer:** Before signing a publishing contract, read it carefully to understand what rights you are giving to the publisher and what this means for your future use of the work. Publishing contracts sometimes require you to assign your copyright to the publisher. An assignment transfers all your copyright rights (other than moral rights) to the publisher. After assigning copyright, you generally cannot print, distribute, post online, or deposit the work in a research repository without the publisher's permission. Moral rights, which include the right to be identified as the author, remain personal to you and cannot be transferred. To enforce your right to be credited, you should assert it in writing in the agreement itself. You are always free to negotiate the terms of a publishing contract before signing. If you want to retain rights, consider giving the publisher a non-exclusive licence to publish rather than assigning copyright outright. Other rights worth negotiating include: the ability to distribute copies to students or colleagues, posting the work to your personal website, including it in a future work of your own, and rights reverting to you if the work is not published. If you want to include published research in a thesis, ensure you retain that right specifically in the agreement, or give a non-exclusive licence only. For open access publication, familiarise yourself with the specific platform's terms as they vary. Tools such as Sherpa Romeo provide information about publisher open access policies by journal.
