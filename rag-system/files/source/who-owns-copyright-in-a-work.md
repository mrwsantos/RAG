# Who owns copyright in a work?

**Question:** Who is the first owner of copyright in a work?


**Answer:** The creator of an original work --- such as the author or artist --- is usually the first owner of any copyright in that work. There are two main exceptions: where a work is created in the course of employment, the employer will own copyright; and where someone commissions and agrees to pay for certain types of work (including a photograph, painting, drawing, diagram, map, chart, plan, engraving, model, sculpture, film, sound recording or computer program), the commissioner will own copyright. Yes. The general ownership rules can be varied by agreement between the parties. The Copyright Act specifically designates the publisher as the owner of copyright in the typographical layout of a published edition. This does not alter the ownership of copyright in the underlying material --- such as the text, illustrations or photographs --- which will generally be owned by the author or artist (or employer or commissioner), unless there is agreement to the contrary. Yes. There may be more than one copyright owner, for example where two or more people collaborate on a work, or where copyright has passed to several beneficiaries under a will. Certain rights in a work can also be assigned to collective management organisations. Yes. Under the Copyright Act, where a person's name appears as author on a published literary work, that person is presumed --- until the contrary is proved --- to be the author and owner of the work.
