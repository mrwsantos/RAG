# The Curious Case of the Mouse and the Pirates

Way back in the ancient history of the 1970s, a small group of
cartoonists lead by Dan O’Neill created an underground comic called “Air
Pirate Funnies.” The Air Pirates were Mickey Mouse antagonists, who drew
Disney characters in very...ahem...adult situations. The publishing of
the comics lead to a 10 year long lawsuit, with Dan O’Neill deliberately
doing whatever he could to provoke Disney.

The Air Pirates viewed Mickey Mouse as a “symbol of conformist
hypocrisy,” so, as a parody, kept his name intact while drawing Mickey
and friends in less than family friendly activities; a blatant and
indisputable copyright and trademark infringement. Of course, Disney
Productions sued the Air Pirates in October 1971, and the next 10 years
was a circus, with multiple legal proceedings, the confiscation of the
10,000 copies of a third issue of the comic book series, the sale of
original artwork (primarily of Disney characters) by the Air Pirates at
comic conventions to raise funds for their legal defence, more content
produced for the Mouse Liberation Front, and a joint smoked in Walt
Disney’s office by Dan O’Neill.

Disney is sometimes viewed as a villain and a bully by artists and
creative people, because of the difficulty of using anything in their
vast resources, without penalty or licensing. But they were once a small
company, and the protection of their intellectual property has been the
bedrock of their success. With copyright protection, and by exercising
all of their creative rights, Disney has been able to exploit their own
creations; turning books into films, into merchandise, into soundtracks,
into art prints, into rides, into candy, into spin-off books...all with
the same full protection that every creator – amateur and professional –
has, by virtue of copyright law.

Disney won, after 10 years of litigation, and not just because they were
the big corporation or a special entity, but because they know the value
of their work and were prepared to protect it. Just so you know, Disney
wasn't in it for the money - the Air Pirates were completely unable to
pay up the more than $2M in fees and penalties, and Disney settled the
case, and promised not to enforce the judgment, as long as the Air
Pirates did not infringe on Disney's copyrights.
