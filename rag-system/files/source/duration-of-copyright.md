# Duration of copyright

**Question:** How long does copyright last in New Zealand?


**Answer:** Copyright lasts for a limited time. Once it expires, a work enters the public domain and can be used freely without permission. The duration depends on the type of work. For literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works, copyright lasts for the life of the author plus 50 years from the end of the year they died. For joint authorship, the 50 years runs from the death of the last surviving author. For sound recordings and films, copyright expires 50 years from the end of the year the work was made or first made available to the public, whichever is later. For communication works, copyright lasts 50 years from the end of the year it was first communicated to the public. For typographical arrangements of published editions, the term is 25 years from the end of the year of publication. For works covered by Crown copyright, protection lasts 100 years from the end of the year the work was made, or 25 years for typographical arrangements. Note that copyright terms in other countries, including Australia, the EU, and the United States, are generally longer at life plus 70 years. A work in the public domain in New Zealand may still be under copyright in another country.
