# When permission is not needed

**Question:** When is permission not required to use copyright material?


**Answer:** Permission is not required when: you are using less than a substantial part of a work; the copyright has expired; you or your organisation owns the copyright; the work is excluded from copyright protection; a statutory exception (permitted use) applies; or you have a free licence or subscription that covers the use. A statutory exception, also known as a permitted use, is a specific circumstance in which copyright material can be used without permission. The main types include: fair dealing (for news reporting, criticism, review, and research or private study); exceptions for educational institutions, libraries and archives; and a collection of ad hoc exceptions for specific circumstances. Fair dealing is a statutory exception that permits the use of copyright material without permission for the purposes of news reporting, criticism or review, and research or private study. New Zealand does not have a broad 'fair use' defence as exists in United States copyright law. A free licence is where a website or creator indicates that you may use their copyright work without needing to ask. It is important to read the Terms of Use or Copyright page to check whether any conditions apply. It should not be assumed that all works on the internet are available under free licences. Creative Commons is a standardised set of copyright licences that some creators use to give particular rights in their digital copyright material to the public without users needing to ask. There are different types of Creative Commons licence with varying rights and restrictions. Not necessarily. Often the use of subscription materials is governed by contract terms which must be adhered to. It cannot be assumed that you can make unlimited use of subscription materials. No. Some other countries allow the use of third party copyright material for the purposes of parody and satire, but there is currently no equivalent copyright exception in New Zealand.
