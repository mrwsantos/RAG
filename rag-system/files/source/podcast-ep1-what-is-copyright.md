# Episode 1: The curious case of two monks and a Psalter

Nau mai, haere mai. Welcome to Know Your Copyrights, the podcast where
we dive into how creative rights work for creative people in Aotearoa,
New Zealand. Ko Karen Workman, tōku ingoa. Ko Taku Mahi ki Copyright
Licensing New Zealand. Ko Te Kaifakahaere for Kapa, Creative Rights
Educator. Over this series, I'll take you through some real stories of
copyright and creative work to help explain the legal stuff and some
practical considerations too. Today's copyright kōrero covers some
general copyright information, but it's not legal advice. If you've got
a copyright problem, the best thing you can do is to get in touch with a
lawyer for their advice on your specific situation. Episode 1. The
curious case of two monks and a salter, or what's the point of
copyright? How much do you think about copyright, really? Chances are,
when you do something like take a screenshot and send it to a friend,
you're not thinking about who owns the image and whether you have
permission. In fact, being honest, even if you regularly make creative
work yourself, you might not think about your own copyright too much
until there's a problem. Perhaps someone takes your work and uses it in
theirs, and then you might find yourself faced with a situation you
don't really know how to respond to. Copyright law has been around a
long time, but it hasn't always been in existence. Think about it.
Before the printing press, copying books was all done by hand and it was
hard work. So taking other people's work wasn't done that much and was
probably a lot harder to track down. There wasn't really a need for
copyright law because there wasn't the wholesale, unauthorized use of
someone else's work. Totally different story today when you can just
tell your phone to take a screenshot. No hands used in the making of
this copy. But essentially, that's what copyright has always been about.
Protection from other people making copies. Which brings us to the first
known recorded copyright case, set in 540 AD with two monks, one
psalter, 3,000 deaths at the Battle of Kul Dremna, otherwise known as
Battle of the Book, and an honourably mentioned cow. Around 540 AD in
Northern Ireland, a monk named Finian founded the Movia Abbey. Finian
had studied in Rome for seven years, was ordained as a priest, and then
returned to Ireland with a copy of St. Jerome's Vulgate, which was
basically a revision of the Bible in Latin by St. Jerome. Very few
people owned books at the time, so the book was of high value and
brought prestige to Finian and his monastery. We're not sure what else
was in his library, but there was a Psalter, a book of Psalms from the
Bible and other devotional material, which is at the center of today's
story. One of Finian's previous and distinguished students, Columba,
stayed at the monastery as a guest in about 560 AD. Finian lent him the
Psalter. Columba really wanted a copy of that book, so he started making
a secret copy. Tradition has it that the copy was made at night in haste
by a miraculous light. But I can imagine it took some time, and probably
some candles, since it was originally 110 pages and would have had to
have been copied by hand. Columba's intention was to take the book with
him when he left the abbey. He'd almost finished the copying when Finian
found him out. Finian demanded Columba hand over the copy, but Columba
refused. Finian took the matter to High King Dermot, and the case was
argued in the king's court. It's not right, Columba said, that the
divine words in that book should perish, or that I or any other should
be hindered from writing them or reading them or spreading them among
the tribes. In his closing address, he told the court that those who
owned the knowledge through books were obligated to spread the knowledge
by copying and sharing them. He felt that to not share knowledge was a
far greater offence than to copy a book that lost nothing by being
copied. High King Darmot ruled in favour of Finian. Now I'm just
guessing, but I suspect that King Darmot might have been swayed to rule
that way for multiple reasons and perhaps copyright wasn't one of them.
He might have decided to align with the monk who had a monastery in his
backyard rather than the visitor. But for whatever reason, King Dermot
gave the win to Finian, saying, To every cow belongs her calf, therefore
to every book belongs its copy. In other words, if you own the book, you
own any copies as well. Columba disagreed with King Dermot's ruling and,
reportedly, in an unstable political climate, instigated a significant
clan rebellion against the High King, causing the crown around 3,000
casualties. It's not reported whether Columba took the book with him
when he was exiled from Ireland after the battle, but Columba's copy is
presumed to be the book we now know as the Katach, or Battle Book, of St
Columba. It is now considered the oldest Irish manuscript and copy of
the Psalter, and the earliest example of Irish writing in existence.
Only 58 of the original 110 pages remain, but that's pretty good for a
book that was invoked before battle as a relic, hanging around the neck
of a chosen monk or holy man as he walked three times around the troops.
Ironically, Finian's original Psalter is lost to history and to the
story. Not surprisingly, the details of copyright law have ebbed and
flowed as technology and artistic practice has changed over time. And,
of course, as the power balance shifts between creators of creative
works, and those who want to use or copy those works. Things like the
printing press, the internet, photocopying, cassette tapes, cameras, all
of these things have impacted the interpretation of and the development
of copyright law all over the world. As technology changes, copyright
law is tested by claims of infringement and rulings in those cases help
us to understand and determine how the law applies in the face of new
technology. One issue I can think of right now is around AI, artificial
intelligence. Some countries define copyright protectable works as those
created with human labor or human endeavor. And so there's a lot of
discussion in those countries around whether an AI-generated work meets
that definition of human labor enough to be given the same rights as,
for example, an oil painting. Today, unlike in 6th century Northern
Ireland, we try to resolve copyright issues without bloodshed or
appealing to a monarch, but sometimes our copyright laws can feel
difficult or overwhelming to navigate. It doesn't really help that the
way that the law gets clarified and shaped is through judgments on real
cases that have been filed and decided in court. In New Zealand, we're
not strongly litigious, meaning we don't sue each other very much, So a
lot of our knowledge and understanding comes from rulings we see in the
news overseas. This is an issue because although the majority of
countries in the world today are members of the Bern Convention, that's
the international agreement governing copyright, we don't all have the
same laws to support the same copyright protection. We have the same
intention, but the detail of each country's laws can be different. For
example, did you know that in the USA, if you copy someone's work to
satirize or parody it without their permission, that's within the scope
of the law? But here in New Zealand, that's not the case. So sometimes
it can be confusing, but stick with me. Now that we've talked about
monks, cows, calves, and the ways in which copyright and copying
technology are entwined, In these episodes, we're going to focus on
giving creators in New Zealand a grounded understanding of their
creative rights and the law as it pertains to us here in Aotearoa. We'll
peel back some concepts, talk about some real cases and situations,
throw in a little history, and hopefully demystify some aspects of
copyright for you.
