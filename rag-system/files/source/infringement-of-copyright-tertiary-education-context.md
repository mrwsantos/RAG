# Infringement of copyright (tertiary education context)

**Question:** What counts as copyright infringement in a tertiary education setting?


**Answer:** Copyright is infringed when someone performs a restricted act in relation to another person's copyright material without permission. In a tertiary education context, the most common concern is copying, which includes photocopying, scanning, downloading, uploading, storing on a computer, and format-shifting (such as converting an MP3 to a CD, which is permitted only if one copy is made). A \"substantial part\" of a work can be very small in quantity: what matters is whether the part taken is important or distinctive, not how much of the work it represents. Copying a few lines of a poem or a paragraph from a journal article may qualify as copying a substantial part. Infringement by authorisation occurs when a person sanctions or approves infringing conduct by others. Employers can be liable for the infringement of employees, and turning a blind eye may amount to authorisation. Institutions should actively discourage use of their systems for infringing purposes. Illegal file sharing via peer-to-peer networks is also addressed by the Copyright Act. Copyright owners can report file sharing to internet service providers, who must send infringement notices. Unresolved cases can be referred to the Copyright Tribunal, which may award up to \$15,000. Individuals, including students and staff, can be personally liable for copyright infringement. Some deliberate infringement may also carry criminal liability.
