# Episode 5: The commissioning rule


I recently went to my sister-in-law's 50th birthday party in the
Wairarapa.

Lovely vintage afternoon tea, little sandwiches, sausage rolls, and of
course cake.

It was great to see old friends, people I hadn't seen for ages and met a
few new ones.

There were speeches and obligatory singing when the cake was cut,

and my sister-in-law's dad was there snapping photos.

If he hadn't brought his fancy camera along,

we might have hired someone to take photos of the event for us.

But if we'd hired a photographer, who would have owned the copyright in
the photos?

Good question. Stick with me. The answer might not be exactly as you
think.

Over the last year, I've been all over the country talking with artists,
authors, and other creative people about copyright.

And this, the law on who owns the copyright when a work, like a series
of photos at a special event, is commissioned, seems to be an area many
people don't know too well.

And, like most legal things, not knowing the rules can have unexpected
and sometimes upsetting consequences.

But let me just start with a reminder that copyright law and the way it
applies depends on the facts of each situation.

So if you're in a dispute or you have another copyright conundrum,
talking to a lawyer who is familiar with copyright law is the very best
thing you could do.

Here in Aotearoa, when you make a creative work, copyright automatically
belongs to you, the person who created it, except if you were
commissioned or employed when you made it.

So what does the law actually say?

The Copyright Act 1994, Section 21, titled Ownership of Copyright,
states that,

Subject to the provisions of this section, the person who is the author
of a work is the first owner of any copyright in the work.

Where an employee makes, in the course of his or her employment, a
literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work,

that person's employer is the first owner of any copyright in the work.

Where a person commissions and pays or agrees to pay for the taking of a
photograph or the making of a computer program,

painting, drawing, diagram, map, chart, plan, engraving, model,
sculpture, film or sound recording,

and the work is made in pursuance of that commission, that person is the
first owner of any copyright in the work.

Loosely and more commonly, the provisions here in Section 21 are known
as the commissioning rule,

which, to recap, means that if you, as a creator, are commissioned or
employed to make something,

and the person commissioning it, or hiring you, pays or agrees to pay
you,

then the owner of the copyright is, drumroll please, not you, but them.

They own the rights to the work.

And that means that they can copy it, manipulate it, use it,

They can basically do whatever they want as if they created it
themselves.

So even though you made it, well, you don't have the right to do those
things.

So, in the case of that photographer my sister-in-law might have hired
for her birthday,

when that photographer decides of her own accord to take pictures of
something,

say, some plants in the gardens,

the copyright in the plant pics belongs to her.

She created that work.

But if my sister-in-law specifically hires her to photograph her party,

then chances are the commissioning rule applies

and the copyright in those photos belongs to my sister-in-law.

Of course, it's not just photos and photographers.

The same is true for other creative disciplines too.

Now, this makes sense, for example,

if you're a journalist employed by a newspaper

or a designer working for a company,

maybe a composer writing music as an employee of an advertising agency

or actually me writing a script for this podcast.

All of my work for Copyright Licensing New Zealand belongs to Copyright
Licensing New Zealand.

And if you write or make creative work in the course of your employment
too,

then that all belongs to the organization that you work for.

Some of us are also creators outside of employed work hours.

I know plenty of people who leave their workday job and go off to
whittle in the tool shed

or pop the headphones on and create another song

or spend all weekend crafting the next great New Zealand novel.

Now, if this creative work is not in the course of your employment,

in other words, if it's done on your own time, at your own expense,

nobody asked you to do it and you're not using it for your job,

then that's yours and you own the rights to it.

Now, if you're in a job where things are a bit blurry or if you're
unsure,

just read your employment contract and make sure there's a clause

that says the creative work you do on your own time belongs to you.

As far as commissioning goes,

it's logical for the rights to belong to the client.

When the work is something like a studio musician hired to play a track

or videography and photography for a private event.

In my sister-in-law's 50th birthday example,

if a photographer was commissioned and retained the rights to the party
pics,

my sister-in-law would have to contact the photographer

every time she wanted to reprint a photo,

turn it into a thank you card,

post it on social media,

or print her favourite memory of the day on a t-shirt.

It makes sense that if someone commissions photos

of an important family party,

the right to do whatever they want with the photos

belongs to the person doing the commissioning.

But here's a slightly different scenario.

Let's say my neighbour, Myrtle,

writes me a letter asking me to paint a picture of her dogs.

I say yes, and we agree on a fee. Myrtle commissions and pays me, and by
the way,

she loves the finished painting. When she commissioned the work, she
only had the one

use in mind, a picture she could hang on her wall. But I see that the
picture has commercial potential,

so I want to upload it and sell it online and make greeting cards and
mugs with it printed on them.

However, because she commissioned the work, Myrtle owns the copyright,
and I, by law,

don't have permission to make copies.

In addition, Myrtle might get a bit upset

if something she thought was a unique piece

and one that features her pets

starts appearing in shops and websites everywhere

without her knowledge.

But what's an artist to do?

We want our multiple streams of income.

In fact, we need them to create any kind of sustainable career.

Let me introduce you to subsection 4 of section 21 of the Copyright Act.

It sets out that commissioning and employment are subject to any
agreement to the contrary.

In other words, the commissioning rule is the default legal position,
but you can create agreements to the contrary.

You can agree that the employer or the person doing the commissioning
does not wholesale own the copyright.

So, in the Myrtle and the Dog painting example, as the artist, I might
use a written agreement to reassign ownership of copyright

so that while Myrtle gets the physical painting, all the rights in the
painting are mine.

Or I might offer Myrtle an exclusive license for the commissioned works,

but retain the right to display the image on my website so that I can
promote my work.

It doesn't have to be an all-or-nothing proposition,

but do use an agreement to clearly set out the conditions of the
commission and its copyright ownership.

What if you're doing the commissioning?

Well, despite the fact that the legal default grants you copyright
ownership,

it's important to make your expectations clear in your commissioning
request.

Set out all the ways you'd like to use the work.

If you want the right to use it as a book cover and as a t-shirt, say
so.

If you want exclusive use of a musical bumper for your podcast

so that nobody else's podcast can use that same music,

make it clear and expect to pay for that exclusivity.

If you intend to use the commissioned work to make money directly by
selling copies,

or indirectly by using it for advertising,

then seek an agreement in writing that gives you that right.

All business relationships are better if they're built on honest, clear
communication.

No matter whether you're the creator or the person commissioning a work,

it is vitally important to get any agreement you make in writing.

Without clear and explicit conditions,

things can come down to a matter of they said this, I said that,

which is rarely a strong legal claim.

Putting your terms into writing is the beginning of the conversation,
not the end.

Everything is up for negotiation, all the way until both parties sign
the agreement.

We're not great at this in New Zealand.

So much is done on trust and handshakes,

and I think we have to reprogram ourselves to see written contracts
differently.

It's my opinion that a written contract or agreement is a way to manaqi
each other,

or to uplift and respect each other, to recognize the value of the mahi
being done

and the value of the relationship between two parties.

It's important that you take time to both create and read contracts
carefully

to make sure everyone understands what's on the table and interprets the
agreement in the same way.

It's not showing weakness to question things that aren't clear to you.

it's wise to make sure that you've interpreted the other party's
position correctly.

In one of my creative rights workshops,

I was told about a particular situation that I think highlights this
really well.

In this case, an artist had created album art for a musician.

They had signed a written agreement allowing the musician to use the
artwork on their album

and in some other promotional material.

However, full copyright ownership of the artwork stayed with the artist.

But this hadn't been the musician's understanding of what was being
agreed when they had signed the contract documents.

Maybe they hadn't read the documents or maybe they hadn't understood the
terms.

Either way, when the artist sold prints of the artwork from their own
website without the album name or text or graphics, the musician felt
wronged.

Yet, when we looked back at the contract, it was clear the artist was
within their rights.

An agreement doesn't have to be in super formal legal language.

But if there's a lot of money, or potentially a lot of money at stake,
and you are able to,

then it's worth spending a little bit up front to have a qualified
person do a contract review,

someone to look for red flags and things to be aware of.

If you're an author or a visual artist, you'll find a link in the
program notes to the International

Authors Forum's very helpful PDFs titled 10 Principles for Fair
Contracts, which are helpful as guidelines.

If you're a member of the New Zealand Society of Authors, you can access
the NZSA's Contract Review Services.

And if you're in another field, it's a good idea to contact the
membership organisation for your creative discipline to see if they
offer similar services.

One final thing to note, payment, or consideration as the lawyers call
it, for a commissioned work doesn't always mean payment in money.

If you both agree to it, it could be providing supplies for your mural,
food, travel, or any other kind of exchange.

And a note, if there's no formal contract or agreement accepting these
sorts of things, you know, supplies and food and travel,

things that are clearly tied to the creative work that you're doing for
commission, that can be enough to imply a contract.

So, let's wrap it up.

The Copyright Act tells us that legally, if you do the work as an
employee or under commission,

the default position is that the rights to your work belong to the
person or the organization

that hired you. You can change that default position by using a clear
and specific agreement

or contract where you agree on the terms of use for the work and the
ownership of copyright.

So next time my sister-in-law holds a birthday party, if she can
convince granddad to make

another appearance with his fancy camera, she could perhaps offer him
dinner, a slice of cake,

or as many tiny sandwiches as he can fit in his pockets and commission
those photos.

Though, of course, I'll be suggesting that they put it all in writing.

That way, when my sister-in-law wants to post the best pics on social

or make a photo t-shirt for our family reunion,

she won't need Grandad's permission.

The rights will be all hers.
