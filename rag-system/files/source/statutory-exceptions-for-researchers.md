# Statutory exceptions for researchers

**Question:** What does fair dealing allow me to do as a researcher or student?


**Answer:** The Copyright Act provides a fair dealing exception for research and private study. This allows you to copy from any type of copyright work, including written works, art, photographs, maps, charts, diagrams, and sheet music, for your own research or study without infringing copyright. The key limitations are: the copy must be for your own personal use only, not for someone else's research or study; and you may only make one copy on any occasion. There is no set amount that may be copied, but a qualitative assessment applies. Copying a small extract for your own research is generally permissible. Copying a whole short work such as a poem, journal article, or photograph may also be fair in some cases. It would never be fair to copy the whole of a published work that is available for purchase. If your copying could affect the copyright owner's ability to earn income from their work, it is unlikely to qualify as fair dealing. Publishing the copied material or placing it in an online repository takes it outside the fair dealing exception for research or study. The Copyright Act also provides fair dealing for criticism or review. This permits use of copyright material in an academic paper, book review, or similar work. Attribution of the author and title of the reviewed work is always required. Unlike fair dealing for research, there is no restriction on the number of copies that can be made for criticism or review, so publication incorporating the reviewed material is not prohibited. As a general guide, copy only what is necessary to make your critical or review point. Incidental copying, such as a painting visible in the background of a documentary film, is not an infringement. However, deliberately including a musical work in a film soundtrack, even incidentally, does require permission. Accidentally capturing part of a radio broadcast during a live recording does not.
