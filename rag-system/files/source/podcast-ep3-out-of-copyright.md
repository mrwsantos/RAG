# Episode 3: Public domain and out of copyright

I've spent half my life in America. It's a place I love.

I've met and worked with some amazing people there, I've driven miles
across the continent,

and I've embraced a whole lot of American words, phrases, and
traditions.

When we talk about copyright here in New Zealand, we often do something
similar and use American

phrases.

But, a bit like when I wish my colleagues a happy Thanksgiving, American
terms don't always

translate in the New Zealand context.

Today, I'm talking about public domain, which is, you guessed it, an
American term that's

become widely used here and all over the world.

You've probably heard it.

It's often used to refer to pretty much any creative work that people
think is free game,

something that no longer has copyright ownership, something that now
anyone can use in whatever

way they want.

It comes with a side of misconception.

I've heard plenty of people say, they posted it on Instagram, so now
it's in the public domain, so I can screenshot and reuse their photo.

Or, it's street art, so it's in the public domain and anyone can take a
photo and turn it into a poster.

But that is not the case.

You might be surprised to hear that America actually adopted the phrase
too, from French law, in the late 1800s.

Before this, in America, anything not protected by copyright or patent
law was called public property or common property or even just commons.

All of these terms were used to describe creative works that have an
expired copyright duration.

But in New Zealand, we use a term that kind of does what it says on the
tin.

When the duration of protection for artistic works has expired, we say
that these works are out of copyright.

That's right. Copyright in a work exists for a set amount of time, and
when that time runs out, then the work is available freely to the public
at large.

Until that time, the creative rights in a work generally belonged to the
copyright owner,

whether that's the individual or the organization that created it,

or someone that the rights have been transferred or sold to.

A good example is The Secret Garden, a classic, well-loved book by
Frances Hodgson Burnett.

In 1995, when it was out of copyright, or in the public domain around
the world,

there was a sudden explosion of new works based on it.

everything you might expect, like new book editions, film and stage
versions,

and a fair few things that you might not, cookbooks, graphic novels,
radio theatre and opera,

musicals and 39 episodes of an anime series.

And because it was out of copyright, all of these things were created

without anyone needing to ask for or gain permission.

In Aotearoa, the creative rights for literary, dramatic, musical and
artistic works are currently protected for the lifetime of the creator,

plus another 50 years from the end of the calendar year of their death.

AI-generated work is protected for 50 years from the end of the year of
creation,

sound recording and film 50 years from the end of the calendar year it
was made in,

or from the end of the year that it was released to the public.

There are other durations set for publishing, communication works like
broadcasts, in this podcast, and crown or government publications.

That means that the copyright owner and their beneficiaries get to
decide how their works will be used for the duration of their lifetime
and then for another 50 years after they've died.

In other countries, it's plus 70 years.

I'm speaking in pretty general terms here because things can get a
little complicated.

The date a work was first published, and the law at that time, can
impact the work's copyright term.

And in New Zealand, our copyright terms are set to change because there
are a few new trade agreements we need to honour.

But more on that later.

There are people who'd like copyright terms to be shorter so that others
have the ability to freely use protected works sooner.

I can understand that desire. I mean, who doesn't want to get the
interesting, cool, beautiful, useful stuff they like for free?

But the cost is creators' ability to earn from the success of their
work, which doesn't seem fair.

And any creator who wants to make their work freely available like this
can put it into the public domain before protection lapses

by using the Creative Commons licensing symbol CC0 to show that they've
pretty much renounced all rights to the work.

From time to time, the duration of copyright pops up in the trade
discussions New Zealand has with other countries.

Recently, it was on the negotiating table when we struck agreements with
the European Union and the United Kingdom, both of which have longer
copyright terms.

One of the principles of the Berne Convention, the International
Agreement Governing Copyright, is national treatment,

which means all signatory countries agree to give foreign works the same
protection they give works created within their own borders, assuming
the other country is also a signatory.

It makes sense to give equal protection to local and overseas work,

and it's understandable that countries wanting to trade with New Zealand

would like us to square up our term of copyright.

Otherwise, it's not really a fair deal.

Right now, the protection New Zealand creators' works get in the UK or
in EU countries

is longer than the term works by creators from those places get here in
New Zealand.

Regardless of where you are and what your local copyright term is,

When that term expires, the creative work goes into the public domain.

You might know January 1st as New Year's Day, but it's also Public
Domain Day.

Every 1st of January, the copyright protection of a whole bunch of works
expires,

putting a fresh batch of previously protected work into the hands of the
public.

This day marks the first day a new, out-of-copyright work is able to be
used by anyone,

no permission needed.

It doesn't matter whether that use is for personal benefit, financial or
otherwise, the work is available.

The whole point of copyright protection is that it gives the creator and
their estate time, for a limited period,

to make choices about the work and its use and the ability to earn from
its success.

And after that, for the rest of history, it's handed over to the public
to use and share and adapt as they would like.

Since every country has its own copyright laws, and terms can differ,

a work can be out of copyright in one country but still protected in
another,

which can make it complicated to figure out whether a work is still
protected and where protection applies.

For example, Albert Einstein's paper on the theory of relativity was
first published in Germany in 1916.

Today, the original paper is in the public domain in the US,

but it will be copyright protected in Germany until the end of 2025, 70
years after Einstein's death.

Because Einstein died in 1955 and it's been more than 50 years since
then,

it's reasonable to assume that, here in New Zealand, as in the US,
Einstein's work is out of copyright.

So, if you've been dreaming of making Theory of Relativity the puppet
show or the board game,

or using excerpts from the paper in your found poetry, you can go right
ahead.

But just for us Kiwis.
