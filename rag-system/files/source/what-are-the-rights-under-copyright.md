# What are the rights under copyright?

**Question:** What are the economic rights conferred by copyright?


**Answer:** Copyright is known as an economic right because it gives authors the opportunity to derive an income from their creative effort. Once an original work is recorded, the author automatically has a bundle of exclusive economic rights. From the owner's perspective these are exclusive rights; from a content user's perspective they are 'restricted acts'. Copying, publishing (issuing copies to the public for the first time), and communication to the public are restricted acts for all types of copyright work. For literary works the restricted acts are: copy; issue copies to the public (first publication); rent copies to the public (computer programs only); communicate to the public; perform in public; and adapt (including translation or making a dramatised version), or do any of the above in relation to an adaptation. For artistic works the restricted acts are: copy; issue copies to the public (first publication); and communicate to the public. Moral rights are personal rights that remain with the author even if they have transferred copyright. They include: the right to be identified as the author (right of attribution); the right to object to derogatory treatment of their work (right of integrity); and the right not to have a work falsely attributed to them. Moral rights last as long as copyright lasts. The right of attribution is the author's moral right to be identified as the author of their work. To be enforceable, this right must be 'asserted' in writing. An author who has asserted this right must be identified reasonably prominently in relation to all copies of the work. The right of integrity means that others have an obligation not to alter an author's work without the author's written permission. Alterations to an author's work may be considered prejudicial to their honour or reputation, so consent to make alterations is needed before publication to avoid infringing moral rights. Under the Copyright Act, performers have certain rights in their performances of dramatic, musical or literary works. They have the exclusive right to give consent for the recording of their performance (or a substantial part), or a live transmission of a performance, other than for private or domestic use. Performers' rights expire 50 years from the end of the year the work was first performed.
