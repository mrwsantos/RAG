# Protecting copyright material

**Question:** How can copyright owners protect their work?


**Answer:** Copyright protection arises automatically in New Zealand without any registration or formalities. However, there are practical steps copyright owners can take to protect their work. Adding a copyright notice to copies of your work is recommended: as a minimum, this should include the copyright symbol, your name, and the year (for example: Copyright symbol, Your Name, 2024). A notice is not legally required but signals ownership and helps people who want to seek permission to contact you. For digital material, copyright management information (CMI) can be embedded in or attached to content to identify the owner and set out terms of use. The Copyright Act protects against intentional removal or alteration of CMI. Digital publishers can also use technological protection mechanisms (TPMs) such as passwords, locking devices, and view-only settings. Supplying devices designed to circumvent TPMs for infringing purposes is an offence under the Act, though circumventing a TPM for a legitimate purpose such as fair dealing is not itself an infringement. New Zealand is a signatory to the Berne Convention, which means NZ copyright works receive equivalent protection in other member countries. If you plan to distribute your work in the United States, there may be advantages to registering with the US Copyright Office to make it easier to bring legal proceedings there if needed. Using clear contractual terms in all dealings involving copyright material is also recommended.
