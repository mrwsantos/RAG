# The works you create

**Question:** Who owns the copyright in work I create as a student or researcher?


**Answer:** In most cases, students and researchers own the copyright in original work they create, including assignments, theses, and dissertations. Copyright arises automatically when the work is written down or recorded, and no notice or symbol is required, though adding one is good practice. As the copyright owner, you have the exclusive right to copy, publish, perform, and communicate your work. Third parties need your permission or must be acting within a legal exception to copy from it. If you are a staff member at a tertiary institution, your employment agreement may affect who owns copyright in work you produce in the course of employment. Under the Copyright Act, an employer generally owns copyright in work created in the course of employment, but this can be varied by contract. If you are contracted to undertake research, the terms of the contract usually determine copyright ownership. In the absence of a contract, the commissioning rule in the Copyright Act may apply. Where you have collaborated with others on a copyright work, you may have joint ownership. It is good practice to agree in advance how ownership will be held and what each party may do with the work. To protect your work, keep dated notes, drafts, and recordings used to produce it. Add your name, the copyright symbol, and the date to your work. You may also formally assert your moral right to be identified as the author in the work itself.
