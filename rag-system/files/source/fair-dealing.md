# Fair dealing

**Question:** What is fair dealing and when does it apply?


**Answer:** Fair dealing is a legal exception that allows copyright material to be used without permission in certain circumstances. In New Zealand it applies to three purposes: research or private study, criticism and review, and news reporting. There is no fixed limit on how much can be copied under fair dealing. The common belief that copying up to 10% automatically qualifies is incorrect. The dealing must fit one of the three purposes and must be fair in the circumstances. Factors considered in assessing fairness include the purpose of the copying, the nature of the work, whether the work was commercially available, the effect on the market for the work, and the amount copied relative to the whole. For research or private study, only an individual doing the copying for their own use can rely on fair dealing. A teacher copying for students does not qualify. Only one copy may be made at a time. For criticism and review, the source must be acknowledged. For news reporting, acknowledgement is required unless the work is a sound recording, film, or communication work. Note that fair dealing for research or study applies only to the act of research or study itself, not to any subsequent publication of that research.
