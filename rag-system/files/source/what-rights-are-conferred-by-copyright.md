# What rights are conferred by copyright?

**Question:** What are the main restricted acts a copyright owner controls?


**Answer:** A copyright owner has the exclusive right to: copy the work; issue copies to the public for the first time; perform, play or show the work in public; communicate the work to the public; and adapt the work. The specific rights differ slightly for different types of material. No. Copyright owners do not have a general 'distribution right'. The publishing right is limited to the first distribution of a work. Once a work has been published, the copyright owner cannot use copyright alone to prevent resale of individual copies (though rental restrictions apply to computer programs, sound recordings and films). When material is published, the publisher acquires a separate copyright in the typographical arrangement of the published edition. This typographical work exists independently from copyright in the underlying works. Publishers have the exclusive right to copy their typographical arrangement, communicate copies to the public, and authorise these activities. No. Just because a publisher has the right to publish a work does not mean they have the right to perform the work in public or do any of the other restricted acts, unless the copyright owner has given their permission. These matters should ideally be addressed in the publishing agreement. No. Moral rights are separate from economic rights. They protect a creator's reputation and the integrity of their work, and remain with the creator even if they have transferred copyright to another person. Moral rights include the right of attribution, the right of integrity, and the right against false attribution.
